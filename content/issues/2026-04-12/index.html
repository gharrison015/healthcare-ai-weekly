Only 4% Hit AI ROI at Scale
Your health system is probably burning cash on AI pilots that will never scale. While 94% of CIOs say AI delays create competitive disadvantage, the math doesn't work for most deployments.
Qventus surveyed 60+ CIOs and senior IT leaders across major health systems and found a brutal reality check. While 94% of healthcare CIOs say AI implementation delays create competitive disadvantage, only 4% of health systems have achieved scaled AI ROI. The disconnect is staggering. Even worse, 22% of IT leaders are still waiting for EHR vendors to build AI features for them, down from 52% in 2025, but that's still one in five executives outsourcing their AI strategy to Epic's roadmap. The report reveals what every consultant knows but few clients want to hear: most AI deployments are expensive pilot theater. Systems are burning cash on proof-of-concepts that look good in quarterly board decks but can't survive contact with real clinical workflows. The 4% that cracked the code aren't necessarily smarter, they're just ruthlessly focused on workflow integration rather than shiny demos. What separates winners from losers isn't the AI model, it's the operational discipline to kill pilots that don't translate to measurable outcomes. For consultants, this is the opening. CFOs are starting to ask hard questions about AI ROI, and most IT leaders don't have good answers.
Risk angle: The pilot theater is expensive. Systems are paying for proof-of-concepts that can't survive contact with real clinical workflows at scale.
Source: Becker's Hospital Review

Anthropic Bets $400M on Biotech
Claude's parent company just made a massive healthcare bet, acquiring stealth biotech startup Coefficient Bio. This signals where Anthropic sees AI's biggest commercial opportunity: not just chatbots, but drug discovery and clinical research.
Anthropic just dropped $400 million on Coefficient Bio, a stealth AI biotech startup that was barely on anyone's radar. This isn't a small bet or a strategic investment, it's a full acquisition that signals where Claude's parent company sees the biggest commercial opportunity in healthcare AI. Coefficient Bio was working on AI-driven drug discovery and molecular design, classic pick-and-shovel AI applications that could generate massive returns if they hit. But here's what makes this interesting: Anthropic isn't just building better chatbots anymore, they're going after the core intellectual property creation in life sciences. For health systems already deploying Claude for clinical documentation or patient communication, this raises questions about roadmap priorities. Is Anthropic going to focus more resources on high-margin biotech applications versus the operational AI tools that hospitals actually need? The timing is also notable. This comes right after Anthropic launched Project Glasswing, their cybersecurity partnership with Nvidia, Microsoft, and other tech giants. They're diversifying aggressively beyond foundation models into vertical applications. For consulting firms, this is a signal that the generic AI conversation is over. Clients need vertical-specific AI strategies, and the major AI vendors are picking their battles.

Epic Pushes Public Health Alerts
Epic is moving beyond individual patient care into population health surveillance. County-level illness alerts could reshape how health systems respond to outbreaks, but also raises questions about data privacy and public health authority.
Epic just rolled out county-level health alerts that monitor illness trends and notify providers when elevated rates are detected. This sounds like a logical extension of their EHR dominance, but it's actually a massive shift in Epic's role from patient record keeper to public health surveillance engine. The feature aggregates data across Epic's installed base to identify disease outbreaks, seasonal trends, and emerging health threats at the county level. For health systems, this creates new capabilities around emergency preparedness and population health management. But it also creates new questions about data governance and privacy. Epic now has real-time visibility into community health patterns that could influence everything from staffing decisions to supply chain management. The bigger story is Epic's evolution from EHR vendor to healthcare intelligence platform. They're not just storing clinical data anymore, they're analyzing it at population scale and pushing insights back to providers. This positions Epic as a potential competitor to public health agencies and traditional epidemiological surveillance systems. For consultants, this is worth tracking because it changes the conversation around Epic implementations. It's not just about clinical workflows anymore, it's about community health intelligence and early warning systems.
Risk angle: Who controls the alert thresholds and what triggers public notification? Epic now has unprecedented visibility into community health patterns across their installed base.

Value-Based Care Watch
CMS Extends Prior Auth to Drugs
CMS wants to expand prior auth interoperability rules to cover pharmaceuticals with shorter decision timeframes. This sounds good but misses the core problem: prior auth is fundamentally broken workflow theater.
CMS proposed extending its prior authorization interoperability framework to cover drugs for the first time, building on the 2024 rule that focused on medical items and services. The proposed rule shortens decision timeframes and expands public reporting requirements. This sounds like progress, but it's missing the point. Prior authorization isn't broken because of interoperability or slow decision times. It's broken because it's fundamentally workflow theater designed to delay and deny care. Making the theater more efficient doesn't fix the underlying problem. The new rule requires payers to decide on standard prior auth requests within 7 calendar days and expedited requests within 2 calendar days, down from current timelines. It also mandates electronic prior auth capabilities and public reporting on approval rates. But here's the trap: shorter deadlines without fixing the underlying process just creates more administrative burden for health systems. They're stuck managing tighter timelines for the same fundamentally adversarial workflow. The real solution isn't faster prior auth, it's eliminating prior auth for evidence-based care. But CMS is treating symptoms instead of the disease. For value-based care organizations, this creates new compliance overhead without addressing the core friction that makes prior auth expensive. The administrative cost per authorization doesn't go down just because the timeline gets shorter.
Risk angle: Shorter deadlines without fixing the underlying process just creates more administrative burden. Health systems get stuck with tighter timelines for the same broken workflow.

Anthropic Partners on Cybersecurity Push
Anthropic launched Project Glasswing with Nvidia, Microsoft, CrowdStrike and others to use AI for cybersecurity vulnerability detection. Their models found 'thousands' of previously unknown bugs, creating new revenue streams beyond foundation models.
Anthropic just launched Project Glasswing, a cybersecurity partnership with Nvidia, Microsoft, CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, and other tech giants to use AI for vulnerability detection and software security. Their latest AI models found 'thousands' of previously unknown bugs in critical software systems, demonstrating real commercial value beyond chatbots. This isn't just another AI partnership announcement. Anthropic is using their Claude models to automatically analyze code for security vulnerabilities, finding bugs that traditional scanning tools miss. The partnership creates new revenue streams for Anthropic while giving cybersecurity vendors AI-powered capabilities they couldn't build internally. For healthcare, this matters because health systems are prime targets for ransomware and data breaches. AI-powered vulnerability detection could identify security holes before attackers exploit them. But it also raises questions about whether health systems can afford enterprise-grade AI security tools when they're still struggling to achieve ROI on basic AI deployments. The timing is notable: this comes right after Anthropic's $400M acquisition of Coefficient Bio, showing they're diversifying aggressively into high-value vertical applications. They're not just competing with OpenAI on foundation models anymore, they're building differentiated products for specific industries. For consulting firms, this creates new opportunities around AI-powered security assessments and helps justify AI investments beyond operational efficiency.

Accenture's global health lead is emphasizing AI governance and intent as the critical factors for scaling AI in healthcare. This creates differentiation against implementation-focused competitors and builds recurring revenue through ongoing governance and monitoring services. For other consulting firms, this is a signal that the AI implementation gold rush is ending and the governance phase is beginning. Health systems need frameworks for AI oversight, not just technical deployment. The firms that build governance expertise now will win the long-term consulting relationships as AI becomes more regulated and complex.

Did You Know?

Gen Z Growing Skeptical of AI

A new Gallup report surveying nearly 1,600 people ages 14-29 reveals a fascinating paradox: Gen Z is increasingly skeptical about AI even as they use it more frequently in educational and workplace settings. The digital-native generation that was supposed to embrace AI is actually becoming more critical as the technology becomes embedded in their daily lives. The survey shows growing concerns about AI's impact on job prospects, academic integrity, and creative authenticity. This matters for healthcare because Gen Z will soon make up a significant portion of the clinical workforce. If the generation that grew up with technology is skeptical about AI, health systems may face more resistance to AI adoption than expected. The findings suggest that successful AI implementation in healthcare will require addressing fundamental trust and value concerns, not just technical capabilities.
Source: The Verge AI

Microsoft Removes Copilot Buttons

Microsoft is starting to remove standalone Copilot buttons from Windows 11 applications, replacing them with more integrated 'writing tools' menus. The change appears in Notepad and Snipping Tool, with the Copilot button disappearing in favor of contextual AI features. This signals a important shift in AI interface design from prominent, branded AI buttons to invisible, integrated assistance. For healthcare technology, this evolution matters because it shows how AI will likely disappear into existing workflows rather than existing as separate tools. The most successful healthcare AI implementations won't be standalone AI applications but AI capabilities embedded directly into clinical workflows, EHRs, and existing software tools that clinicians already use.
Source: The Verge AI
